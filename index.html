0) Core Concepts & Definitions

Vaccine vs Immunoglobulin (Serum)

  • Vaccine (active immunization): stimulates the immune system to produce antibodies; takes time to work but provides longer protection.
  • Immunoglobulin / Serum (passive immunization): provides immediate ready-made antibodies; short-term protection; used to bridge the time until vaccine-induced immunity develops.

PrEP vs PEP

  • PrEP (Pre-Exposure Prophylaxis): rabies vaccination given before exposure for higher-risk individuals.
  • PEP (Post-Exposure Prophylaxis): rabies vaccination given after exposure (bite/scratch/saliva to wound or mucosa).

1) Rabies — Overview (Raw)

1.1 What is rabies?

Rabies is a viral disease (Lyssavirus) affecting the central nervous system. Once clinical symptoms develop, rabies is almost always fatal—so prevention is critical.

1.2 Transmission

  • Virus is present in saliva of infected animals.
  • Exposure routes: bites, scratches that break the skin, or saliva contacting open wounds or mucous membranes (eyes/mouth).

1.3 Incubation period

Often 1–3 months, but can be shorter or longer depending on location of exposure (closer to CNS often shorter), severity, viral inoculum, and host factors.

1.4 Clinical course (reference)

  • Early: fever, malaise, headache; tingling/pain at exposure site.
  • Later: hydrophobia, aerophobia, agitation, confusion, paralysis → coma/death.

2) Rabies — Immediate Actions After Bite/Scratch

Immediate wound care (CDC concept)
  • Begin all rabies PEP with immediate and thorough cleansing with soap and water.
  • If available, irrigate with a virucidal agent (e.g., povidone-iodine).
  • Do not delay medical assessment after a risky exposure.
  • Wound closure decisions vary; priority is cleansing + exposure risk management.

3) Rabies PrEP (Pre-Exposure Prophylaxis) — Detailed

3.1 Who may need PrEP (examples)

CDC risk-category examples typically include: laboratory work with rabies virus, frequent bat contact/high-density bat environments, many veterinary/wildlife professions, and certain travelers to dog-rabies endemic areas with limited access to care (risk-based).

3.2 PrEP schedule (CDC/ACIP)

PrEP schedule
  • 2-dose IM series: Day 0 and Day 7

3.3 Risk-based maintenance (raw reference)

  • Risk category 1: 2-dose series + titer checks every 6 months.
  • Risk category 2: 2-dose series + titer checks every 2 years.
  • Risk category 3 (sustained risk > 3 years): 2-dose series + either one-time titer check (year 1–3) OR 1-dose booster (3 weeks to 3 years after first vaccine in the 2-dose series).
Key point
  • PrEP does not remove the need for PEP after exposure.
  • Previously vaccinated people receive shorter PEP and do not get RIG.

4) Rabies PEP (Post-Exposure Prophylaxis) — Full Details

4.1 Key decision: previously vaccinated vs not

Rabies PEP differs depending on prior rabies vaccination status. Management typically includes wound care, rabies vaccine, and sometimes rabies immunoglobulin (RIG/HRIG) depending on scenario.

4.2 Not previously vaccinated (CDC)

ComponentDetails
Wound care Immediate cleansing with soap/water; povidone-iodine if available.
Vaccine IM 1.0 mL on Days 0, 3, 7, 14.
Immunocompromised Add a 5th vaccine dose on Day 28.
HRIG Used as indicated (see HRIG section).
Injection site Deltoid (adults); anterolateral thigh acceptable for children; no gluteal.

4.3 Previously vaccinated (CDC)

ComponentDetails
RIG / HRIG Do not administer.
Vaccine IM 1.0 mL on Days 0 and 3.
Injection site Deltoid (adults) or anterolateral thigh (children); no gluteal.
Contraindications / pregnancy / age (CDC concept)
  • No known contraindications for rabies vaccination when PEP is needed.
  • Pregnancy is not a contraindication for rabies PEP.
  • Suitable for all age groups including infants and children.

5) HRIG / Rabies Immunoglobulin — Full Detail

What HRIG does
  • Provides neutralizing antibodies at the exposure site until vaccine-mediated antibodies develop.
Dose (CDC)
  • 20 IU/kg for all age groups (including children).
Administration (CDC)
  • Infiltrate as much as feasible directly into/around the wound.
  • Any remaining dose: IM at a site distant from vaccine injection site.
Key warnings (CDC)
  • Do not administer HRIG in the same syringe as vaccine.
  • Do not exceed the recommended dose.
  • Separate anatomical locations for HRIG vs vaccine to avoid neutralization at the same site.
Timing window if missed initially (CDC)
  • If HRIG wasn’t given on Day 0, it can be administered up to and including Day 7 of the PEP series.
Previously vaccinated
  • HRIG is not given to previously vaccinated persons.

6) WHO Intradermal (ID) Rabies PEP Regimens — Extra Reference

  • One-week, 2-site ID: 0.1 mL on Days 0, 3, 7.
  • If previously immunized: 1-site ID 0.1 mL on Days 0 and 3.
  • Category III exposures: immunoglobulin + vaccine + immediate wound washing.
  • Choice of IM vs ID depends on local protocol, product labeling/approval, and trained staff.

7) Tetanus — Overview (Raw)

7.1 What is tetanus?

Tetanus is an acute, potentially fatal disease caused by Clostridium tetani, an environmental bacterium whose spores are widely present in soil, dust, and animal feces. The clinical disease is mainly toxin-mediated (tetanospasmin), affecting the nervous system and leading to muscle rigidity and painful spasms.

Key prevention principle
  • No person-to-person transmission. Risk is related to wound contamination + favorable conditions (deep puncture/devitalized tissue/anaerobic environment).

8) Tetanus — Incubation, Forms, Clinical Notes

8.1 Incubation period (and what it predicts)

  • Commonly cited incubation: typically around 3–21 days (average ~8 days in CDC materials); shorter incubation tends to correlate with more severe disease.
  • Neonatal tetanus: symptoms often appear 4–14 days after birth (average about 7 days) in reference materials.
  • Cephalic tetanus: incubation may be very short (e.g., 1–2 days in some summaries).

8.2 Clinical forms (classification)

  • Generalized tetanus (most common)
  • Localized tetanus (uncommon; can progress to generalized)
  • Cephalic tetanus (rare; cranial nerve involvement; can progress to generalized)
  • Neonatal tetanus (public health relevance; linked to maternal immunization and clean delivery/cord care)

8.3 Generalized tetanus — typical clinical picture (reference)

  • Often begins with trismus (lockjaw), followed by neck stiffness, dysphagia, abdominal rigidity.
  • Autonomic instability may occur (e.g., sweating, elevated BP, tachycardia episodes).
  • Spasms can persist for weeks; recovery may take months.

9) Tetanus — Wound Risk + Post-Injury Prevention Workflow

Rust is not the cause of tetanus. It is a proxy for a typical scenario: a dirty object causing a puncture wound. The real risk factors include spore contamination + conditions favoring germination/toxin production (deep puncture, devitalized tissue, anaerobic environment).

9.2 Wound categories used in decisions (CDC style)

CategoryMeaningTypical examples / risk logic (raw)
Clean & minor wounds Low tetanus risk. Superficial, clean cuts; minimal contamination; minimal tissue damage.
Dirty or major wounds Higher tetanus risk (especially if devitalized tissue). Puncture wounds; wounds contaminated with dirt/soil/feces/saliva; crush injuries; burns; compound fractures; wounds with necrotic tissue; etc.

9.3 Post-injury prevention workflow (structured)

Step 1 — Wound care
  • Clean all wounds.
  • Remove dirt/foreign material.
  • Remove/debride necrotic or devitalized tissue when present.
Step 2 — Antibiotics note
  • Antibiotic prophylaxis specifically to prevent tetanus after an injury is generally not recommended in CDC-style guidance; however, infected wounds should be treated promptly.
Step 3 — Decide on tetanus vaccination (CDC-style rules)
  • No vaccine needed (regardless of wound type) if BOTH:
    • Last tetanus vaccination < 5 years ago, AND
    • Primary series completed.
  • Vaccination recommended:
    • All wounds: unknown history / unvaccinated / incomplete primary series.
    • Clean & minor: complete series + last dose ≥ 10 years.
    • Dirty/major: complete series + last dose ≥ 5 years.
Step 4 — Decide on TIG (Tetanus Immune Globulin) prophylaxis
  • TIG is never indicated for clean & minor wounds.
  • For dirty/major wounds, TIG is typically indicated for:
    • Unknown vaccination history
    • Never vaccinated
    • Incomplete primary series
    • HIV or severe immunodeficiency (raw reference logic)
  • Prophylaxis dose often referenced in CDC guidance: 250 IU IM.

9.4 “Guide table” logic (Pink Book style)

  • If wound is not clean/minor AND immunization history is unknown or fewer than 3 tetanus toxoid doses → give TIG + tetanus toxoid–containing vaccine (raw rationale: TIG provides temporary antitoxin while vaccine builds longer immunity).
  • Some guide tables highlight TIG consideration in immunocompromised states even if prior vaccination exists (raw note for team reference).

10) Tetanus Vaccines — Types, Routine Schedules, Boosters

10.1 Vaccine types (raw reference list)

  • DT, DTaP, Td, Tdap (and combination products containing DTaP antigens).
  • High-level difference: some contain pertussis components (DTaP/Tdap) while DT/Td do not; antigen quantities differ between pediatric vs adolescent/adult formulations (raw note).

10.2 Routine childhood schedule (DTaP core — raw)

  • Primary series commonly listed: 2, 4, 6 months.
  • Boosters: 15–18 months and 4–6 years.
  • Some schedules note: if dose 4 is given on/after 4th birthday, the 5th dose may be optional (raw schedule note).

10.3 Boosters (adolescents/adults)

Booster logic
  • Antitoxin levels decline over time → boosters commonly recommended every 10 years.
  • For wound management, a booster may be needed earlier depending on wound risk (commonly the 5-year rule for dirty/major wounds).

10.4 Contraindications & precautions (raw reference notes)

  • Contraindications (general): severe allergic reaction (anaphylaxis) to a vaccine component or following a prior dose; encephalopathy within 7 days after a pertussis-containing vaccine not attributable to another cause (applies to pertussis-containing products).
  • Precautions (examples): moderate/severe acute illness; progressive/unstable neurologic disorder; Guillain-Barré syndrome within 6 weeks after a prior tetanus-toxoid vaccine; history of Arthus-type hypersensitivity reaction (defer toxoid vaccination until at least 10 years since last tetanus-toxoid vaccine).
  • Some guidance notes: if a high-risk wound exists, the risk of withholding vaccine may outweigh risk of administering it even during illness (raw note).
  • Local reactions (pain/redness/swelling) are common; fever may occur; severe systemic reactions are rare.
  • Arthus-type reactions are rare but relevant to booster timing decisions.

11) TIG — Prophylaxis vs Treatment (Dose Differences)

Important distinction
  • Prophylaxis TIG (after injury, if indicated): commonly referenced dose 250 IU IM.
  • Treatment TIG (actual tetanus disease): some references recommend a single IM dose of 500 units, sometimes with part infiltrated around the wound if identifiable (raw Pink Book-style note).
  • Rationale: TIG helps neutralize unbound toxin; it does not reverse toxin already bound to nerve endings.

Hospital management (raw, high-level)

  • Airway/ventilation support may be needed (spasms can compromise breathing).
  • Sedation and spasm control; ICU-level monitoring in severe cases.
  • Wound debridement and infection management as clinically appropriate.
